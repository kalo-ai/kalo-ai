Course Objectives

This course covers deep learning methods for 3D computer graphics and 3D computer vision. It introduces students to a wide range of problems related to the processing and creation of visual content, including 3D models, scenes, animations, and images. For each problem, the course describes state-of-the-art learning-based solutions based on recent research findings. Topics include 3D deep learning, 3D object recognition, segmentation, correspondences, 3D object and scene reconstruction, image synthesis, 3D shape synthesis, animation, and neural rendering. Students review research papers, and complete 5 programming assignments in Python/PyTorch. Students taking the 674 section will also work on an open-ended course project related to the topics covered in this course.

Course Prerequisites

CMPSCI graduate students: there are no prerequisites for CMPSCI graduate students, although familiarity with probability, statistics, linear algebra, undergraduate computer graphics and machine learning is essential!

Undergrads and others: the course has the following prerequisites:
- Introduction to Computer Graphics (CMPSCI 373) OR Machine Learning (COMPSCI 589) OR Neural Networks (COMPSCI 682) with grade B or better.

This course counts as a CS Elective toward the CMPSCI major (BA/BS) and can satisfy a second or third AI core requirement in the CMPSCI PhD and MSc program. It offers 3 credits.

Week-by-week outline (tentative)

Week 1: Image, Shape, Scene Representation
Week 2: Introduction to Neural Networks
Week 3: 3D Deep Learning: the multi-view approach
Week 4: 3D Deep Learning: the voxel-based approach
Week 5: 3D Deep Learning: the point-based approach
Week 6: 3D Deep Learning: the mesh-based approach
Week 7: Introduction to Deep Generative Models (GANs, VAEs, Diffusion)
Week 8: 3D generative models: generating views, voxels, points
Week 9: 3D generative models: generating implicits and primitives
Week 10: 3D generative models: generating meshes and scenes
Week 11: Generative models for rigging and animation
Week 12: Neural Photorealistic Rendering
Week 13: Neural Artistic Rendering

Should you take 574 or 674?

Both 574 and 674 sections meet at the same time, same location, and have access to the same lectures and course material. The programming assignments are the same. The difference is that 574 requires students to present an existing research paper towards the end of the course (~15 min presentation), while 674 requires students to develop on their own a deep learning method related to the topics of the course as a final course project. The project can be an implementation of a (small-scale) research idea/prototype, or could lead to a submission of a research paper in the future. The project plan must be first discussed with the instructor (soon after the middle of the semester). Students also present their project work towards the end of the course. As a result, 674 requires more effort and is more targeted for students interested in graphics+vision research.
